About Benchmarking

Benchmarking is an effective, intuitive method to assess and compare businesses’ performance.

Responsible 100 operates a collaborative process of grouping examples of what businesses are doing, on each of the responsibility issues we examine, into one of four ‘buckets’, either: POOR, OKAY, GOOD or EXCELLENT. 


Scorecards 

Responsible 100 scorecards are dynamic records of POOR, OKAY, GOOD and EXCELLENT standards of business policy and practice. 

In addition to actual examples of what businesses are doing out in the world, sometimes examples of GOOD and EXCELLENT standards of policy and practice can also be the product of the imagination of members of our people’s assembly.  

Scorecards provide an intuitive and transparent way to assess performance. And through continuous contribution from businesses and supporters alike, they form pools of shared and open-sourced knowledge. 

Scorecards are currently created and developed in a few different ways. Irrespective, their purpose is always to serve as constantly updated, living benchmarks by which to understand and measure responsibility performance. 

Some responsibility issues are easier to break down into our four distinct standards of business performance than others. Sometimes it is useful to apply a performance yardstick in creating and developing a scorecard. Sometimes an impact yardstick is more useful, sometimes using both lenses is useful.

This table indicates these two ways to think about POOR, OKAY, GOOD and EXCELLENT on almost any given responsibility issue: 

Score Performance Yardstick Impact Yardstick
EXCELLENT The responsibility issue is fundamental to the business; and/or rigorous approaches to pursuing best practices are in place; and/or excellence on the issue is of strategic importance to the businessThe business is net positive through its response to, or pursuit of best practices on, this issue; any negative impacts on people and planet stemming from the business’s policies and practices in respect of or response to this issue, if any are created, are significantly offset by its positive impacts
GOODThe business demonstrates a commitment to tackling or responding to the responsibility issue and has a number of appropriate policies and practices in place
The business has significant positive impacts through its response to, or pursuit of best practices on, this issue, these positive impacts do not (yet) outweigh the negative impacts the business is responsible for in respect of this issue it is however heading towards net neutrality
OKAYThe business does some good things in respect of the responsibility issue but on an ad hoc basis; and/or the business continues with some poor practices; the issue is not a priority for the business; the issue is not consistently or materially relevant to the business (from an objective / neutral standpoint)
The business has both positive and negative impacts through its response to, or pursuit of best practices on, this issue, however the business is net negative in respect of these impacts with some way to go before nearing net neutralit
POORThe business performs below its own expectations and/or those of its stakeholders in respect of this issue; and/or the business has no relevant policies or practices in place to respond better; and/or the business pursues or perpetuates various poor practices
The business is offloading costs onto society and/or the environment, and/or exploiting vulnerable resources or stakeholder groups; it has significant negative impacts with very few or no positive impacts; it is distinctly net negative

Scorecard Development

Scorecards are developed and improved as part of an on-going, collaborative and defined process:  

Identification - Some issues burst onto our collective consciousness in a flash. Climate activism such as striking and Black Lives Matter provide two recent examples. Anyone can propose exploring new issues.  

Research - Typically, Responsible 100 will research any proposed new issue initially to assess if and how its inclusion may provide additional value and to minimise any potential overlap with other related issues (and their associated scorecards) that we already explore.  

Partnership - Proposed new issues and early drafts of new scorecards are shared with the people’s assembly and experts in the field (e.g. academics, business leaders and practitioners, NGOs, thank tanks). These are invaluable ways of gaining wider input and refining draft scorecards.  

Testing in the real world - New scorecards are tested with businesses keen to benchmark against the new issue as soon as possible. Businesses assessing against new scorecards are encouraged to provide feedback to identify anything that is not working, or is missing, or any additional aspects.

Steps 2-4 are iterated. And new scorecard/issue proposals are received all the time.
